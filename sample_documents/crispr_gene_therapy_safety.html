Safety and Tolerability of CTX110 (CRISPR Edited CAR-T Cells) in B-Cell Malignancies

Clinical Context

CTX110 is an investigational, allogeneic, CRISPR-Cas9 gene-edited CAR-T cell therapy designed to target CD19-positive cells in patients with relapsed or refractory B-cell malignancies, including diffuse large B-cell lymphoma (DLBCL).

Gene Editing Specifications

  • TCR Disruption: The T-cell receptor (TCR) alpha constant (TRAC) locus is disrupted using CRISPR-Cas9 to prevent graft-versus-host disease (GvHD).
  • B2M Deletion: The beta-2 microglobulin (B2M) gene is deleted to eliminate major histocompatibility complex (MHC) class I expression, reducing host-mediated immune rejection of allogeneic cells.

Safety Results Summary

In the Phase 1 trial involving 32 patients, CTX110 demonstrated a safety profile comparable to autologous CAR-T therapies, with lower rates of severe systemic toxicities:

  • Cytokine Release Syndrome (CRS): Occurred in 38% of patients, with only 1 patient (3%) experiencing a Grade 3 or higher CRS event.
  • Immune Effector Cell-Associated Neurotoxicity Syndrome (ICANS): Neurotoxic events occurred in 12% of patients, all resolving within 72 hours of dexamethasone administration.
  • Graft-Versus-Host Disease: No cases of GvHD were reported, validating the efficiency of CRISPR-mediated TRAC gene disruption.

Conclusion

Allogeneic gene-edited T-cells can be safely administered to heavily pre-treated leukemia/lymphoma patients without causing immunological GVHD reactions.
